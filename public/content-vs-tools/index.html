Law Of The Instrument

Thu, May 7, 2015

Have you ever read a blog where the first (and often, only) post is one about how the author set up the blog? This is a typical problem for programmers. It’s a case of solving the easy problem, the one they know how to, as opposed to the real probem, which is the one that matters - to actually write something. A little more than a month ago, I made a similar mistake.

This March, I introduced Unfolds in a blog post. Unfolds is on the order of 500 LOCs and 60 commits, but despite being my main hacking project for about a month, it fails to test anything useful.

Ironically, I knew the problem I was trying to solving was mostly a content problem, as opposed to a tool problem, yet I attacked it by writing a tool. Can tools help? Absolutely, but that is a separate question, and it wasn’t my guiding principle when thinking about the problem. It doesn’t matter if what you are building is clever if it doesn’t solve a real problem.

Outside of this meta-lesson, did I learn anything? Absolutely, but I would have learned a lot more if what I built was guided by the problem. As a basic example to illustrate my point: why was I setting up a Datomic database when just keeping everything in-memory would’ve been fine?

What would a real hypothesis look like?

The idea behind Unfolds is still potent, and there are many questions and hypotheses hiding in it. Here are a few sketches, and some thoughts on them.

  1. If you understand an idea, can you get the gist of it across in less than 200 words? More precisely: is there any standard configuration (i.e. not individualized) of a limited set of words, such that reading them will be enough in answering the basic questions a person has while trying to understand an idea?

  2. If you only have a limited amount of time, does reading the first 200 or so words of Wikipedia satisfy 1 above? Can we do better than Wikipedia on the metric we outlined?

  3. Can this author explain 10 concepts himself under 200 words? How does understanding the concepts deeply play into the choice of words? Essentially it’s a compression problem, and I suspect knowledge of the source material is vital.

  4. Can we find a test for what makes a good explanation? A friend of mine wisely suggested that removing specific examples would be tempting, but most likely wrong.

  5. Can we create a tool that somehow encourages clear and concise writing? Examples: use of limited vocabulary, auto-suggestions of links.

  6. How much of a difference does links make? If we can bootstrap an explanation using other, possibly subordinate, explanations, this would help with the compression a lot. In fact, this is in a sense how language works.

  7. What about images and illustrations? Which types of illustrations are useful for understanding, and which are vital? For example, maps are distinctly superior to any verbal-only explanation you could come up with. On the other hand, there’s a trade-off in editable and time investment.

I’m sure there are many more, but these are things on the top of my head that’d be interesting to investigate. Some are easier to test than others, and I’ll probably revisit the matter soon.

Lessons?

Aside from the problem domain, I think the lesson here is simple but trite: If all you have is a hammer, you will treat everything as a nail, but with the added twist that this is true even if you are aware of it.

So what can we do about it? I think there are a few things. First of all, sometimes it’s ok - maybe if I kept naively working on Unfolds it would have lead me somewhere interesting.

Secondly, by releasing something before it’s ready you get some time to let reality kick in and reflect.

And lastly, by using a stricter framework, such as, in my case, thinking about it in terms of an experiment with a falsifiable hypothesis, we can make real progress, faster. Luckily, I was born after the scientific revolution when this idea became widespread, so I didn’t have to figure this out myself, and neither do you.
